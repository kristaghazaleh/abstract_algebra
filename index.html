Overview

This page is meant to work as dense study material and a long-term reference. The recurring theme in abstract algebra is that most problems are not solved by brute force computation. They are solved by knowing exactly which definition applies, which standard theorem is relevant, and what must be proved in what order. A lot of the work is pattern recognition: equivalence relation questions ask for three properties, subgroup questions ask for a closure-plus-inverse test, homomorphism questions ask for the operation-preserving law, and quotient questions ask whether normality and well-definedness have been handled correctly.

What to think first

  • Relation problem: check reflexive, symmetric, transitive, then identify the classes.
  • Operation problem: ask whether the operation is actually binary, then closure, associativity, identity, inverses.
  • Subset problem: ask whether this is a subgroup and use a subgroup test instead of redoing all axioms.
  • Order problem: think powers, divisibility, Lagrange, and least positive exponent.
  • Permutation problem: rewrite in disjoint cycles and compose right to left.
  • Map problem: compute \(\varphi(xy)\) and \(\varphi(x)\varphi(y)\) directly.
  • Quotient problem: ask whether the subgroup is normal and whether the operation on cosets is well defined.

Very short checklist of facts that solve a lot of exercises

  • Kernels are normal subgroups.
  • Images of homomorphisms are subgroups.
  • Every subgroup of an abelian group is normal.
  • A subgroup of index \(2\) is normal.
  • Every cyclic group is abelian, and every subgroup of a cyclic group is cyclic.
  • The order of an element divides the order of a finite group.
  • A group of prime order is cyclic.
  • Two groups cannot be isomorphic if they differ on abelian/nonabelian behavior, element orders, subgroup counts, or cyclicity.

Core Definitions

These are the exact meanings that get used over and over. In abstract algebra, many proofs fail not because the big idea is wrong, but because one word was being used loosely. Keeping these definitions sharp makes the later theorems easier to use correctly.

Sets, relations, and classes

  • Relation on a set \(S\): a subset of \(S imes S\). Writing \(a \sim b\) means \((a,b)\) is in that subset.
  • Equivalence relation: a relation that is reflexive, symmetric, and transitive.
  • Equivalence class of \(a\): \([a] = \{x \in S : x \sim a\}\).
  • Partition of a set: a collection of nonempty pairwise disjoint subsets whose union is the whole set.
  • Well defined: the output does not depend on the choice of representative. This matters whenever elements are represented in more than one way, especially in quotient constructions.

Operations and groups

  • Binary operation on \(G\): a function \(\ast : G imes G o G\). The phrase “on \(G\)” already includes closure.
  • Group: a set \(G\) with a binary operation that is associative, has an identity element, and gives every element an inverse.
  • Abelian group: a group whose operation is commutative: \(ab = ba\) for all \(a,b \in G\).
  • Identity element: an element \(e\) with \(eg = ge = g\) for every \(g \in G\).
  • Inverse of \(g\): an element \(g^{-1}\) with \(gg^{-1} = g^{-1}g = e\).
  • Order of a group \(G\): the number of elements in \(G\), written \(|G|\), when \(G\) is finite.
  • Order of an element \(g\): the least positive integer \(n\) such that \(g^n = e\), if such \(n\) exists; otherwise the element has infinite order.

Subgroups and cyclic groups

  • Subgroup \(H \le G\): a subset \(H\) of \(G\) that is itself a group under the same operation as \(G\).
  • Proper subgroup: a subgroup \(H\) with \(H e G\).
  • Trivial subgroup: the subgroup \(\{e\}\).
  • Cyclic subgroup generated by \(g\): \(\langle g angle = \{g^n : n \in \mathbb{Z}\}\) in multiplicative notation, or \(\{ng : n \in \mathbb{Z}\}\) in additive notation.
  • Cyclic group: a group generated by one element.
  • Generator: an element \(g\) such that \(\langle g angle = G\).

Permutations and symmetry groups

  • Permutation of a set \(X\): a bijection from \(X\) to itself.
  • Symmetric group \(S_n\): the group of all permutations of \(\{1,2,\dots,n\}\).
  • Cycle: a permutation that moves a chosen list of elements cyclically and fixes everything else.
  • Disjoint cycles: cycles with no symbols in common; disjoint cycles commute.
  • Transposition: a 2-cycle.
  • Even permutation: a permutation expressible as a product of an even number of transpositions.
  • Odd permutation: a permutation expressible as a product of an odd number of transpositions.
  • Alternating group \(A_n\): the subgroup of \(S_n\) consisting of all even permutations.
  • Dihedral group \(D_n\): the group of symmetries of a regular \(n\)-gon, including rotations and reflections.

Cosets and counting language

  • Left coset of \(H\) in \(G\): a set of the form \(gH = \{gh : h \in H\}\).
  • Right coset of \(H\) in \(G\): a set of the form \(Hg = \{hg : h \in H\}\).
  • Index of \(H\) in \(G\): the number of left cosets of \(H\) in \(G\), written \([G:H]\).
  • Conjugate of an element \(b\) by \(x\): the element \(xbx^{-1}\).
  • Conjugacy class of \(b\): the set \(\{xbx^{-1} : x \in G\}\).

Maps and quotients

  • Homomorphism \(\varphi : G o G'\): a map satisfying \(\varphi(xy) = \varphi(x)\varphi(y)\) for all \(x,y \in G\).
  • Kernel of \(\varphi\): \(\ker \varphi = \{g \in G : \varphi(g)=e'\}\), where \(e'\) is the identity in the codomain.
  • Image of \(\varphi\): \(\operatorname{im}\varphi = \varphi(G) = \{\varphi(g): g \in G\}\).
  • Isomorphism: a bijective homomorphism.
  • Automorphism: an isomorphism from a group to itself.
  • Normal subgroup \(N rianglelefteq G\): a subgroup such that \(gN = Ng\) for every \(g \in G\); equivalently \(gNg^{-1}=N\) for every \(g \in G\).
  • Quotient group \(G/N\): the set of cosets of \(N\) with multiplication \((aN)(bN)=(ab)N\), defined only when \(N\) is normal.
  • Direct product \(G imes H\): the set of ordered pairs with componentwise operation.

Common quick examples

  • Equivalence relation: congruence mod \(n\) on \(\mathbb{Z}\), where \(a \sim b\) means \(n\mid(a-b)\).
  • Non-example of an equivalence relation: the relation \(a < b\) on \(\mathbb{R}\), since it is not reflexive and not symmetric.
  • Cyclic example: \(\mathbb{Z}_{12}\) is generated by \(\bar 1\), and also by \(\bar 5\) and \(\bar 7\).
  • Non-cyclic example: \(\mathbb{Z}_2 imes \mathbb{Z}_2\) has no element of order 4, so no single element generates the whole group.
  • Kernel example: \(\varphi : \mathbb{Z} o \mathbb{Z}_n\) given by \(\varphi(k)=\bar k\) has kernel \(n\mathbb{Z}\).
  • Normal subgroup example: every subgroup of an abelian group is normal, so every subgroup of \(\mathbb{Z}_{12}\) is normal.
  • Non-normal example: in \(S_3\), the subgroup \(\langle (12) angle\) is not normal because conjugating \((12)\) by another permutation can produce a different transposition.

How to find counterexamples from definitions

When a statement built from definitions is false, the fastest counterexample usually comes from breaking exactly one required condition while keeping the others mostly intact. If a relation is claimed to be an equivalence relation, try to violate just one of reflexive, symmetric, or transitive. If a set is claimed to be a group, try to break closure or inverses before looking for something exotic. If a subset is claimed to be cyclic, compare it to familiar noncyclic examples like the Klein four group. If a statement uses words like all, every, or always, test the smallest standard objects first: \\(\mathbb Z\\), \\(\mathbb Z_n\\), \\(S_3\\), \\(D_4\\), and \\(V_4=\mathbb Z_2 \times \mathbb Z_2\\).

  • Break closure: odd integers under addition fail because odd + odd = even.
  • Break cyclicity: \\(V_4\\) is abelian, every nonidentity element has order \\(2\\), and no single element generates the whole group.
  • Break normality: use a non-normal subgroup of \\(S_3\\), such as \\(\langle (12)\rangle\\), and conjugate it.
  • Break isomorphism guesses: compare element orders, abelian vs. nonabelian behavior, subgroup counts, or sizes of centers.

Sets, Relations, Equivalence Classes, and Well-Definedness

Equivalence relations

A relation \(\sim\) on a set \(S\) is an equivalence relation if it is reflexive, symmetric, and transitive. These three properties are not optional details; they are exactly what makes it possible to group elements into classes that behave like the same object for the purpose of the problem.

  • Reflexive: for every \(a\in S\), show \(a\sim a\).
  • Symmetric: if \(a\sim b\), show \(b\sim a\).
  • Transitive: if \(a\sim b\) and \(b\sim c\), show \(a\sim c\).

Equivalence classes

The equivalence class of \(a\) is \([a]=\{x\in S : x\sim a\}\). Distinct equivalence classes are disjoint, and the equivalence classes partition the set. That means every element belongs to exactly one class. In practice, many algebraic constructions are built from equivalence classes rather than raw elements.

Standard examples

  • Congruence modulo \(n\): \(a\equiv b \pmod n\) iff \(n\mid(a-b)\).
  • Same absolute value on \(\mathbb{R}\): \(x\sim y\) iff \(|x|=|y|\).
  • Rational-number construction from integer pairs: \((a,b)\sim(c,d)\) iff \(ad=bc\).

Common examples to know cold

  • On \(\mathbb{Z}\), the relation \(a\sim b\iff a\equiv b\pmod 5\) has exactly five classes: \([0],[1],[2],[3],[4]\).
  • On \(\mathbb{R}\), the relation \(x\sim y\iff x-y\in\mathbb{Z}\) packages real numbers by their fractional part. A typical class is \([0.3]=\{\dots,-1.7,-0.7,0.3,1.3,2.3,\dots\}\).
  • For fractions, \((1,2)\sim(2,4)\sim(3,6)\) because they represent the same rational number. This is the standard model for why well-definedness matters.

Well-defined operations

When the elements of the new structure are equivalence classes, you must prove that any operation on those classes does not depend on the representative chosen. This is what well defined means. It is one of the most important proof ideas in the course. For example, on \(\mathbb{Z}_n\), to define multiplication by \([a][b]=[ab]\), you must show that if \(a\equiv a'\pmod n\) and \(b\equiv b'\pmod n\), then \(ab\equiv a'b'\pmod n\). If that fails, then the operation is not legitimate on classes.

How to prove well defined

  • Start by assuming two representatives describe the same class.
  • Translate that assumption into the defining relation.
  • Apply the proposed operation to both representatives.
  • Show the outputs are equivalent under the same relation.

How to find counterexamples in relations and well-definedness

For relations, the easiest counterexamples come from tiny sets and explicit ordered pairs. To disprove reflexivity, find one element \\(a\\) with \\(a\not\sim a\\). To disprove symmetry, find \\(a\sim b\\) but not \\(b\sim a\\). To disprove transitivity, build a chain \\(a\sim b\\) and \\(b\sim c\\) with \\(a\not\sim c\\). Standard counterexample relations often come from inequalities or parity statements that only partially behave like equality.

  • Not symmetric: on \\(\mathbb Z\\), define \\(a\sim b\\) if \\(a\le b\\). Then \\(1\sim 2\\) but \\(2\not\sim 1\\).
  • Not transitive: on \\(\mathbb Z\\), define \\(a\sim b\\) if \\(|a-b|\le 1\\). Then \\(0\sim 1\\) and \\(1\sim 2\\), but \\(0\not\sim 2\\).
  • Not well defined on cosets or classes: choose two different representatives of the same class and show the output changes. For example, a map on \\(\mathbb Z_n\\) that depends on the integer representative rather than its residue class is usually not well defined.
  • Partition failure: if classes overlap without being equal, the relation is not inducing a partition the right way.

Whenever a problem says an operation or map is defined on equivalence classes, the counterexample strategy is almost always the same: replace a representative by an equivalent one and see whether the result lands in the same class.

Groups

Definition

  • Group axiom package: a group is not just a set with an operation; the operation must already be a binary operation on the set, then it must be associative, have an identity, and give every element an inverse.
  • Important precision: closure is built into the phrase “binary operation on \(G\).” If an operation can send two elements of the set outside the set, then it is not even an operation on that set.
  • Common exam example: \((\mathbb{Z},+)\) is a group with identity \(0\) and inverse of \(n\) equal to \(-n\). By contrast, \((\mathbb{Z},\cdot)\) is not a group because most integers do not have multiplicative inverses in \(\mathbb{Z}\).

Binary operation and closure

The first hidden issue in many problems is whether the proposed operation is even binary. The output must stay in the set. A set with an operation that leaves the set is not a group before any deeper axiom is even considered.

Basic facts used constantly

  • The identity element is unique.
  • Each element has a unique inverse.
  • Left and right cancellation hold.
  • \((ab)^{-1}=b^{-1}a^{-1}\).
  • \((a^{-1})^{-1}=a\).
  • If \(a^2=e\) for every \(a\in G\), then \(G\) is abelian.
  • If \(x^2=x\), then \(x=e\).

How to show something is not a group

  • Find one failure of closure.
  • Give one concrete failure of associativity.
  • Show no identity exists.
  • Show some element has no inverse in the set.

Common examples

  • \((\mathbb{Z},+)\), \((\mathbb{R},+)\), and \((\mathbb{C}^\times,\cdot)\) are groups.
  • \((\mathbb{Z},\cdot)\) is not a group because most elements have no multiplicative inverse in \(\mathbb{Z}\).
  • Invertible matrices form a group under multiplication, but all matrices do not.

Common examples that show up on tests

  • \((2\mathbb{Z},+)\) is a group under addition even though it is not all of \(\mathbb{Z}\). The identity is still \(0\), and the inverse of \(2k\) is \(-2k\).
  • \((\mathbb{Z}_n,+)\) is always a finite cyclic group generated by \([1]\).
  • \((U(n),\cdot)\), the units mod \(n\), is a group under multiplication mod \(n\). This is a common place where closure and inverses have to be checked in modular arithmetic.
  • \((\mathbb{R}\setminus\{0\},\cdot)\) is a group, but \((\mathbb{R},\cdot)\) is not because \(0\) has no inverse.

How to find counterexamples in group questions

To disprove that a structure is a group, do not check all axioms. Hunt for the cheapest failure. Closure and inverses are the fastest. Associativity is usually expensive to disprove unless the operation is artificial, so only go there after easier checks fail.

  • No identity: positive integers under addition have closure and associativity, but \\(0\\) is missing, so there is no additive identity.
  • No inverses: nonzero integers under multiplication have identity \\(1\\), but most elements have no multiplicative inverse inside the set.
  • Not closed: odd integers under addition are not closed since odd + odd = even.
  • Associativity trap: subtraction on integers fails because \\((a-b)-c\\) and \\(a-(b-c)\\) are usually different.

If a statement claims every group with some property is abelian or cyclic, try the smallest nonabelian groups first: \\(S_3\\), \\(D_3\\), and \\(D_4\\). Small orders often settle the question immediately.

Subgroups, Cyclic Groups, Generators, and Orders

Subgroup tests

  • Definition: \(H \le G\) means \(H\subseteq G\) and \(H\) is a group under the same operation as \(G\).
  • Two-step subgroup test: show \(H\neq\varnothing\), then show closure under the operation and inverses.
  • One-step subgroup test: if \(H\neq\varnothing\) and \(ab^{-1}\in H\) for all \(a,b\in H\), then \(H\le G\).
  • Finite subgroup test: if \(H\) is a nonempty finite subset of \(G\) and is closed under the operation, then \(H\le G\).
  • Common exam examples: \(n\mathbb Z\le (\mathbb Z,+)\), \(\langle a\rangle\le G\), and \(\{a+b\sqrt2:a,b\in\mathbb Z\}\le (\mathbb R,+)\).

Theorems and corollaries for cyclic groups

  • Theorem: Every cyclic group is abelian. If \(G=\langle a\rangle\), then every element has the form \(a^m\), and \(a^m a^n = a^{m+n} = a^{n+m} = a^n a^m\).
  • Theorem: Every subgroup of a cyclic group is cyclic.
  • Corollary: Every subgroup of \((\mathbb Z,+)\) has the form \(n\mathbb Z\) for some \(n\ge 0\).
  • Corollary: If \(|G|=p\) is prime, then every nonidentity element generates \(G\), so \(G\) is cyclic.
  • Corollary: A cyclic group of infinite order is isomorphic to \(\mathbb Z\), and a cyclic group of order \(n\) is isomorphic to \(\mathbb Z_n\).
  • Corollary: A cyclic group of order \(n\) has exactly one subgroup of order \(d\) for each positive divisor \(d\mid n\).

Generators and how to find them

A group is cyclic if one element generates every element of the group. In multiplicative notation \(\langle a\rangle = \{a^n:n\in\mathbb Z\}\). In additive notation \(\langle a\rangle = \{na:n\in\mathbb Z\}\). In finite cyclic groups, generator questions are usually really gcd questions.

  • Theorem: If \(a\) has order \(n\), then \(|a^k| = n/\gcd(n,k)\).
  • Corollary: In \(\mathbb Z_n\), the element \([k]\) is a generator exactly when \(\gcd(k,n)=1\).
  • Corollary: The number of generators of a cyclic group of order \(n\) is \(\varphi(n)\).
  • Practical use: To list generators of \(\mathbb Z_n\), list the classes \([k]\) with \(1\le k\le n\) and \(\gcd(k,n)=1\).
  • Practical use: To prove an element does not generate \(\mathbb Z_n\), show \(\gcd(k,n)\neq 1\), or compute its order and see that it is smaller than \(n\).

Order of an element

The order of an element \(a\) is the least positive integer \(n\) such that \(a^n=e\), if such \(n\) exists. If no such positive integer exists, then \(a\) has infinite order. In additive notation, it is the least positive \(n\) with \(na=0\).

  • Theorem: The order of \(a\) is exactly \(|\langle a\rangle|\).
  • Theorem: If \(G\) is finite and \(a\in G\), then \(|a|\mid |G|\) by Lagrange, since \(|a|=|\langle a\rangle|\).
  • Corollary: In a group of order \(15\), possible element orders are only \(1,3,5,15\).
  • Product groups: In \(G\times H\), the order of \((g,h)\) is \(\operatorname{lcm}(|g|,|h|)\) when the component orders are finite.
  • Permutation groups: For a permutation written as disjoint cycles, its order is the least common multiple of the cycle lengths.

Common exam examples

  • In \(\mathbb Z_{12}\), \([8]\) has order \(12/\gcd(12,8)=3\), so \(\langle [8]\rangle=\{[0],[8],[4]\}\).
  • In \(\mathbb Z_{18}\), the generators are exactly \([1],[5],[7],[11],[13],[17]\), since these are the residues relatively prime to \(18\).
  • The group \(\mathbb Z_2\times \mathbb Z_2\) is abelian but not cyclic because every nonidentity element has order \(2\), so no element has order \(4\).
  • If a finite group has no proper nontrivial subgroups, then it is cyclic of prime order: choose \(a\neq e\), then \(\langle a\rangle\) cannot be proper.

How to find counterexamples for subgroups, cyclic groups, and orders

When a subset is claimed to be a subgroup, try to show it fails identity, inverses, or closure under the ambient operation. For cyclic-group statements, the best counterexamples come from direct products such as \\(\mathbb Z_2 \times \mathbb Z_2\\), because many plausible statements fail there while the group stays small and easy to compute with.

  • Subset not a subgroup: positive even integers in \\(\mathbb Z\\) are closed under addition, but they do not contain \\(0\\), so they are not a subgroup.
  • Finite abelian does not imply cyclic: \\(V_4=\mathbb Z_2 \times \mathbb Z_2\\) is abelian but not cyclic.
  • Same order does not imply same subgroup: in \\(S_3\\), the three subgroups generated by transpositions all have order \\(2\\) but are distinct.
  • Order dividing \\(|G|\\) does not imply existence: Lagrange says subgroup orders must divide \\(|G|\\), but the converse fails in general. A standard example is \\(A_4\\), which has order \\(12\\) but no subgroup of order \\(6\\).
  • Element order test: if every nonidentity element has small order, the group may fail to be cyclic even if its size is moderate.

For false statements about generators in \\(\mathbb Z_n\\), pick an element \\(k\\) with \\(\gcd(k,n)\ne 1\\). Then \\(k\\) cannot generate all of \\(\mathbb Z_n\\).

Permutation Groups, Cycle Notation, Alternating Groups, and Dihedral Groups

Permutation basics

A permutation of a set \(A\) is a bijection from \(A\) to itself. The permutations of \(\{1,2,\dots,n\}\) form the symmetric group \(S_n\) under composition. Composition is read right to left: apply the rightmost permutation first.

  • Rewrite into cycle notation whenever possible.
  • Disjoint cycles commute.
  • The inverse of \((a_1\,a_2\,\dots\,a_k)\) is \((a_k\,\dots\,a_2\,a_1)\).
  • A \(k\)-cycle has order \(k\).
  • The order of a permutation is the least common multiple of the lengths of its disjoint cycles.

Main theorems for cycle notation

  • Theorem: Every permutation can be written as a product of disjoint cycles.
  • Corollary: The decomposition into disjoint cycles is unique up to reordering the disjoint cycles and cyclic rotation inside each cycle.
  • Theorem: A \(k\)-cycle can be written as a product of \(k-1\) transpositions: \[ (a_1\,a_2\,\dots\,a_k)=(a_1\,a_k)(a_1\,a_{k-1})\cdots(a_1\,a_2). \]
  • Corollary: Every permutation is a product of transpositions.
  • Theorem: The order of a permutation is the least common multiple of the lengths of the disjoint cycles in its decomposition.

Transpositions, sign, and parity

A transposition swaps two entries and fixes the rest. Every permutation can be written as a product of transpositions, but the important theorem is that the parity of the number of transpositions does not depend on the chosen decomposition. That is what makes even and odd permutations legitimate algebraic notions.

  • Theorem: No permutation can be written both as a product of an even number of transpositions and as a product of an odd number of transpositions.
  • Corollary: The sign map \(\operatorname{sgn}:S_n\to\{\pm 1\}\) is a well-defined homomorphism.
  • Corollary: The alternating group \(A_n\), consisting of the even permutations, is a normal subgroup of \(S_n\) of index \(2\).
  • Practical use: A \(k\)-cycle is even exactly when \(k-1\) is even, so odd-length cycles are even permutations and even-length cycles are odd permutations.

Conjugation and cycle type

If \(\mu=(x_1\,x_2\,\dots\,x_k)\) and \(\sigma\in S_n\), then \[ \sigma\mu\sigma^{-1}=(\sigma(x_1)\,\sigma(x_2)\,\dots\,\sigma(x_k)). \] So conjugation just relabels the entries in the cycle.

  • Theorem: Conjugation preserves cycle type.
  • Corollary: Two permutations in \(S_n\) are conjugate if and only if they have the same cycle structure.
  • Practical use: To determine conjugacy classes in \(S_n\), sort permutations by cycle type.

Dihedral groups

The dihedral group \(D_n\) is the symmetry group of a regular \(n\)-gon. It has \(n\) rotations and \(n\) reflections, so \(|D_n|=2n\). A standard presentation is \[ D_n=\langle r,s\mid r^n=e,\ s^2=e,\ srs=r^{-1}\rangle. \] Every element can be written either as \(r^k\) or \(sr^k\).

  • Theorem: The rotation subgroup \(\langle r\rangle\) is cyclic of order \(n\).
  • Corollary: \(\langle r\rangle\) has index \(2\), so it is normal in \(D_n\).
  • Corollary: Every reflection has order \(2\), since \((sr^k)^2=e\).
  • Practical use: To multiply in \(D_n\), push \(s\) past powers of \(r\) using \(sr=r^{-1}s\).

Common exam examples

  • \((1\,2\,3)(1\,2)=(1\,3)\) when composition is read right to left.
  • The order of \((1\,4\,2)(3\,5)\) is \(\operatorname{lcm}(3,2)=6\).
  • The permutation \((1\,2\,3)\) is even because \((1\,2\,3)=(1\,3)(1\,2)\).
  • In \(S_4\), permutations of cycle type \((2,2)\) are all conjugate to one another.
  • In \(D_5\), every nontrivial rotation generates the rotation subgroup because \(\langle r\rangle\cong \mathbb Z_5\).

How to find counterexamples in permutation and dihedral problems

Permutation counterexamples are easiest when you work in cycle notation. To disprove a claim, choose a permutation with the smallest cycle type that breaks it. Two-cycles and three-cycles already control a lot of the theory.

  • Products do not commute: in \\(S_3\\), \\((12)(23)=(123)\\) but \\((23)(12)=(132)\\).
  • Equal order does not mean conjugate in an arbitrary group: this is true in \\(S_n\\) only via cycle type, not as a general group fact.
  • Same sign does not mean equal: \\((12)(34)\\) and \\((13)(24)\\) are both even, but they are different permutations.
  • Not every subgroup of \\(S_n\\) is normal: in \\(S_3\\), \\(\langle(12)\rangle\\) is not normal.
  • Dihedral groups are usually nonabelian: in \\(D_n\\) for \\(n\ge 3\\), reflections and rotations fail to commute since \\(sr=r^{-1}s\\).

When a claim involves parity, try one transposition. When it involves cycle order, compare a single \\(k\\)-cycle to a product of disjoint cycles. When it involves dihedral structure, test one rotation \\(r\\) and one reflection \\(s\\).

Cosets, Equivalence by a Subgroup, and Lagrange's Theorem

Cosets and the subgroup equivalence relation

  • Definition: if \(H\le G\) and \(g\in G\), then \(gH=\{gh:h\in H\}\) is a left coset and \(Hg=\{hg:h\in H\}\) is a right coset.
  • Equivalence relation: define \(a\sim b\) if \(a^{-1}b\in H\). Then the equivalence classes are exactly the left cosets of \(H\).
  • Practical use: This is why cosets partition the group: they are equivalence classes.

Core coset theorems

  • Theorem: For any \(a\in G\), the map \(H\to aH\) given by \(h\mapsto ah\) is a bijection. Therefore every left coset has the same number of elements as \(H\).
  • Theorem: Two left cosets are either equal or disjoint.
  • Equivalent criterion: \(aH=bH\iff a^{-1}b\in H\iff b\in aH\).
  • Corollary: The set of left cosets of \(H\) partitions \(G\).
  • Warning: Left cosets and right cosets need not be the same unless \(H\) is normal.

Lagrange's theorem

If \(G\) is finite and \(H\le G\), then \[ |G|=[G:H]|H|. \] The proof is exactly the coset partition argument: \(G\) breaks into \([G:H]\) disjoint left cosets, and each coset has \(|H|\) elements.

  • Corollary: The order of every subgroup divides the order of the group.
  • Corollary: The order of every element divides the order of the group, since \(|a|=|\langle a\rangle|\).
  • Corollary: If \(|G|=p\) is prime, then \(G\) is cyclic.
  • Corollary: If \(H\le G\) and \([G:H]=2\), then \(H\trianglelefteq G\).
  • Corollary: If \(a\in G\) and \(|G|=n\), then \(a^n=e\) whenever \(|a|\mid n\). This is the finite-group exponent fact that gets used constantly in examples.

What Lagrange does and does not give you

  • It does give: divisibility restrictions on subgroup orders and element orders.
  • It does not give: existence of a subgroup of every divisor order.
  • It does not give: existence of an element of every divisor order.
  • Typical use: eliminate impossible subgroup sizes fast, then use additional structure to prove existence or nonexistence.

Normality and cosets

  • Theorem: \(H\trianglelefteq G\) if and only if \(gH=Hg\) for all \(g\in G\).
  • Equivalent test: \(H\trianglelefteq G\) if and only if \(gHg^{-1}=H\) for all \(g\in G\).
  • Practical use: If a quotient is mentioned, the first thing to check is whether the subgroup is normal.

Common exam examples

  • In \((\mathbb Z,+)\) with \(H=3\mathbb Z\), the cosets are \(0+H,1+H,2+H\). These are exactly the congruence classes mod \(3\).
  • If \(|G|=12\) and \(|H|=4\), then \([G:H]=3\), so there are exactly three left cosets.
  • In \(\mathbb Z_{12}\), the subgroup \(\langle \bar4\rangle=\{\bar0,\bar4,\bar8\}\) has index \(4\), and the cosets are obtained by adding \(\bar0,\bar1,\bar2,\bar3\).
  • If \(|G|=21\), then no subgroup of order \(8\) can exist because \(8\nmid 21\).
  • In any abelian group, left and right cosets always agree because every subgroup is normal.

How to find counterexamples with cosets and Lagrange

Coset counterexamples usually come from using a subgroup that is not normal or from confusing element statements with subgroup statements. The smallest useful laboratory is \\(S_3\\). Its subgroup \\(H=\langle(12)\rangle\\) gives left and right cosets that are different, so many false claims collapse there immediately.

  • Left cosets need not equal right cosets: take \\(G=S_3\\), \\(H=\langle(12)\rangle\\). Then some left cosets differ from right cosets, showing \\(H\\) is not normal.
  • Divides does not imply exists: if someone claims every divisor of \\(|G|\\) occurs as a subgroup order, use \\(A_4\\) with no subgroup of order \\(6\\).
  • Same number of cosets does not force isomorphism: index is only a counting invariant, not a full structure invariant.
  • Coset multiplication may fail: if \\(H\\) is not normal, \\((aH)(bH)=abH\\) can depend on representatives, so quotient multiplication is not well defined.

Whenever a quotient-style statement looks suspicious, try to break well-definedness by choosing two representatives from the same coset and multiplying them. If the products land in different cosets, that is the counterexample.

Direct Products and Finite Abelian Group Structure

Direct products

If \(G_1,\dots,G_n\) are groups, then \(G_1\times\cdots\times G_n\) is a group under componentwise operation. The identity is the tuple of identities and inverses are taken componentwise.

  • \(|(g_1,\dots,g_n)|=\operatorname{lcm}(|g_1|,\dots,|g_n|)\) when the component orders are finite.
  • The product is abelian iff each factor is abelian.
  • A product of cyclic groups need not be cyclic.

When is a product cyclic?

A very common fact is that \(\mathbb{Z}_m\times\mathbb{Z}_n\) is cyclic if and only if \(\gcd(m,n)=1\). More generally, the lcm of the component orders must be large enough to equal the group order if one element is going to generate everything.

Finite abelian groups

Finite abelian groups are built from products of cyclic prime-power groups. In practical problem solving, this means that to list possible abelian groups of order \(n\), first factor \(n\) into prime powers, then write all partitions of each prime-power exponent. Each choice gives one isomorphism type.

Practical examples of classification thinking

  • Order \(8\): \(\mathbb{Z}_8\), \(\mathbb{Z}_4\times\mathbb{Z}_2\), \(\mathbb{Z}_2\times\mathbb{Z}_2\times\mathbb{Z}_2\).
  • Order \(12\): abelian possibilities are \(\mathbb{Z}_{12}\) and \(\mathbb{Z}_6\times\mathbb{Z}_2\), which is isomorphic to \(\mathbb{Z}_3\times\mathbb{Z}_2\times\mathbb{Z}_2\).
  • Order \(24\): separate the \(2^3\) part and the \(3\) part, then combine the possible \(2\)-primary pieces with \(\mathbb{Z}_3\).

How to show two groups are not isomorphic

  • Compare whether they are abelian.
  • Compare whether they are cyclic.
  • Compare numbers of elements of each order.
  • Compare subgroup structure.

Common exam examples

  • \(\mathbb{Z}_4\not\cong \mathbb{Z}_2\times\mathbb{Z}_2\) because \(\mathbb{Z}_4\) has an element of order \(4\), while \(\mathbb{Z}_2\times\mathbb{Z}_2\) does not.
  • \(\mathbb{Z}_6\cong \mathbb{Z}_2\times\mathbb{Z}_3\) because \(\gcd(2,3)=1\) and both groups are cyclic of order \(6\).
  • \(S_3\not\cong \mathbb{Z}_6\) because one is nonabelian and the other is abelian.

How to find counterexamples for direct products and abelian-group claims

Direct-product counterexamples are powerful because they let you keep complete control of element orders coordinatewise. The standard source is \\(\mathbb Z_m \times \mathbb Z_n\\).

  • Product of cyclic groups need not be cyclic: \\(\mathbb Z_2 \times \mathbb Z_2\\) is not cyclic. More generally, \\(\mathbb Z_m \times \mathbb Z_n\\) is cyclic only when \\(\gcd(m,n)=1\\).
  • Same order, different structure: \\(\mathbb Z_4\\) and \\(\mathbb Z_2\times\mathbb Z_2\\) both have order \\(4\\) but are not isomorphic because one has an element of order \\(4\\) and the other does not.
  • Abelian does not force unique decomposition from raw appearance: compare groups through invariant factors or elementary divisors, not by superficial notation.
  • Order counts alone are too weak: groups of the same size can differ in numbers of elements of each order, numbers of subgroups, or cyclicity.

If you need a counterexample to an isomorphism claim, compute orders of a few elements first. That usually settles the question faster than trying to construct a map.

Group Homomorphisms, Kernels, Images, and Isomorphisms

Homomorphism definition

  • Definition: a homomorphism preserves the group operation: \(\varphi(xy)=\varphi(x)\varphi(y)\) for all \(x,y\in G\).
  • What this does not mean: it does not automatically preserve ordinary addition or multiplication unless those are the group operations in the groups being used.
  • Common exam example: \(\varphi:\mathbb Z\to\mathbb Z_n\) given by \(\varphi(k)=\bar k\) is a homomorphism because \(\varphi(a+b)=\overline{a+b}=\bar a+\bar b\).
  • Another common example: determinant \(\det:GL_n(\mathbb R)\to \mathbb R^\times\) is a homomorphism because \(\det(AB)=\det(A)\det(B)\).

Automatic identities and structural theorems

  • Theorem: \(\varphi(e_G)=e_{G'}\).
  • Theorem: \(\varphi(x^{-1})=\varphi(x)^{-1}\).
  • Theorem: \(\varphi(x^n)=\varphi(x)^n\) for every integer \(n\).
  • Theorem: The image of a subgroup under a homomorphism is a subgroup.
  • Theorem: The kernel of a homomorphism is a normal subgroup of the domain.
  • Corollary: \(\varphi\) is injective if and only if \(\ker\varphi=\{e\}\).

Kernel and image

The kernel is \(\ker\varphi=\{x\in G:\varphi(x)=e_{G'}\}\), and the image is \(\operatorname{im}\varphi=\{\varphi(x):x\in G\}\). These two objects capture most of the useful information in a homomorphism problem.

  • If you know the kernel, you know exactly which elements are identified with the identity.
  • If you know the image, you know exactly which elements of the codomain are actually reached.
  • For quotient questions, kernels are often the normal subgroup you want.

Isomorphisms and quotient structure

An isomorphism is a bijective homomorphism. Isomorphic groups preserve element orders, subgroup structure, normality, cyclicity, abelian behavior, and many counting features. If one of these invariants differs, the groups are not isomorphic.

  • First isomorphism theorem: for any homomorphism \(\varphi:G\to G'\), \[ G/\ker\varphi \cong \operatorname{im}\varphi. \]
  • Corollary: \(\mathbb Z/n\mathbb Z\cong \mathbb Z_n\) via the natural projection \(\mathbb Z\to \mathbb Z_n\).
  • Corollary: Since \(\ker(\operatorname{sgn})=A_n\), we get \(S_n/A_n\cong \mathbb Z_2\).

Common homomorphism examples

  • The sign map \(\operatorname{sgn}:S_n\to\{\pm1\}\) has kernel \(A_n\).
  • The determinant map \(\det:GL_n(\mathbb R)\to\mathbb R^{\times}\) has kernel \(SL_n(\mathbb R)\).
  • The exponential map \(\varphi:(\mathbb R,+)\to(\mathbb R_{>0},\cdot)\) given by \(\varphi(x)=e^x\) is an isomorphism.
  • If \(G=\langle a\rangle\) is cyclic and \(\varphi:G\to G'\) is a homomorphism, then \(\varphi\) is determined completely by the single value \(\varphi(a)\).

How to find counterexamples for homomorphisms and isomorphisms

To show a map is not a homomorphism, the quickest move is to choose simple elements \\(a,b\\) and compare \\(\varphi(ab)\\) with \\(\varphi(a)\varphi(b)\\). You only need one failure. To show a bijection is not an isomorphism, find a structural property not preserved by the map or by the groups themselves.

  • Not a homomorphism: \\(\varphi:\mathbb Z\to\mathbb Z\\) given by \\(\varphi(n)=n+1\\) is not additive because \\(\varphi(m+n)=m+n+1\\) but \\(\varphi(m)+\varphi(n)=m+n+2\\).
  • Injective but not surjective: \\(\varphi:\mathbb Z\to\mathbb Z\\), \\(\varphi(n)=2n\\).
  • Surjective but not injective: reduction mod \\(n\\), \\(\mathbb Z\to\mathbb Z_n\\).
  • Bijection but not isomorphism: any bijection between \\(\mathbb Z_4\\) and \\(V_4\\) fails to be an isomorphism because element orders disagree.
  • Kernel test: if a supposed injective homomorphism has a nontrivial kernel, that alone is a counterexample to injectivity.

For false claims about images, test whether the image is actually closed under the target operation. For false claims about kernels, remember the kernel is always a normal subgroup, so any non-normal candidate cannot be a kernel.

Normal Subgroups and Quotient Groups

Normal subgroup tests

  • Definition: \(N rianglelefteq G\) means \(N\le G\) and \(gN=Ng\) for all \(g\in G\).
  • Equivalent conjugation test: \(N rianglelefteq G\) iff \(gNg^{-1}=N\) for all \(g\in G\).
  • Elementwise version: it is enough to show \(gng^{-1}\in N\) for every \(g\in G\) and \(n\in N\).
  • Common exam example: in any group, \(\ker\varphi\) is normal. In \(S_3\), \(A_3\) is normal, but \(\langle(12) angle\) is not.

Quick sources of normality

  • Every subgroup of an abelian group is normal.
  • Every kernel is normal.
  • Every subgroup of index \(2\) is normal.

Quotient groups

If \(N\trianglelefteq G\), then the set of left cosets \(G/N\) becomes a group under \[ (aN)(bN)=(ab)N. \] Normality is exactly what makes this operation well defined. Without normality, different choices of representatives can produce different answers.

What to remember about quotients

  • The elements of \(G/N\) are cosets, not elements of \(G\).
  • The identity element is the coset \(N\).
  • The inverse of \(aN\) is \(a^{-1}N\).
  • If \(G\) is finite, then \(|G/N|=[G:N]=|G|/|N|\).

Quotient intuition

Taking a quotient means collapsing all elements of the same coset into one object. In effect, the subgroup \(N\) becomes the identity in the quotient. This is why quotient groups and homomorphisms are so closely related: a homomorphism identifies elements that differ by something in its kernel.

Common exam examples

  • \(\mathbb{Z}/n\mathbb{Z}\) is the standard quotient: all integers that differ by a multiple of \(n\) become the same element.
  • Since \(A_3\trianglelefteq S_3\) and \(|S_3:A_3|=2\), the quotient \(S_3/A_3\) has order \(2\), so it is isomorphic to \(\mathbb{Z}_2\).
  • If \(N\trianglelefteq G\) has index \(3\), then \(|G/N|=3\), so the quotient is cyclic of prime order.
  • In an abelian group \(G\), every subgroup \(N\) is normal, so quotients \(G/N\) always make sense.

How to find counterexamples for normality and quotient groups

The central counterexample method here is to use a subgroup that is not normal. Again, \\(\langle(12)\rangle\\) inside \\(S_3\\) is the standard one. If normality fails, quotient multiplication usually fails too, and that gives a direct counterexample to many quotient claims.

  • Subgroup not normal: in \\(S_3\\), \\((123)(12)(123)^{-1}=(23)\notin \langle(12)\rangle\\).
  • Quotient undefined: if \\(H\\) is not normal, different representatives of the same cosets can produce different products, so \\((aH)(bH)\\) is not well defined.
  • Normal does not imply central: a normal subgroup need not commute elementwise with the whole group.
  • Same quotient size does not imply same quotient behavior: structure matters, not just number of cosets.

If a statement says a quotient exists, first ask whether the subgroup is normal. If not, you already have the correct obstruction. If yes, then the next possible counterexample target is well-definedness of the induced operation or map.

Proof Patterns and Problem-Solving

Equivalence relation proofs

State the relation carefully. Then prove reflexive, symmetric, and transitive in that order. If one of them fails, stop and give a clean counterexample instead of wandering through extra algebra.

Group proofs

First show the operation is binary. Then check closure and associativity. Then identify the identity. Then solve for inverses. If the operation comes from something already known to be associative, say that explicitly.

Subgroup proofs

Use the subgroup test. Let \(a,b\in H\). Show \(ab^{-1}\in H\). If you can, also say why \(H\neq\varnothing\). In many proofs this is the fastest correct path.

Order arguments

Ask what the least positive exponent is that returns the identity. For permutations, use disjoint cycles. For products, use least common multiples. For finite groups, use divisibility from Lagrange.

Isomorphism arguments

To prove groups are isomorphic, define a candidate map and verify the homomorphism law. Then prove bijectivity. To prove groups are not isomorphic, compare invariants and look for a mismatch.

Homomorphism arguments

Compute \(\varphi(xy)\) and compare it directly to \(\varphi(x)\varphi(y)\). After that, determine the kernel and image. Many follow-up questions become automatic once those are known.

Normality and quotient arguments

If a quotient appears, the first sentence should usually address normality. If normality is true, then justify that the coset product is well defined. If the subgroup is not normal, then the quotient group does not exist.

When a problem asks for all possibilities

  • Use divisibility restrictions from Lagrange.
  • Use cyclic subgroup structure if the group is cyclic.
  • Use element orders and subgroup counts to rule cases out.
  • For finite abelian groups, use the classification into cyclic prime-power factors.

How to build counterexamples systematically

There is a pattern behind most valid counterexamples. Start by identifying the exact hypothesis that would have to fail. Then pick the smallest standard group or relation where that failure is easy to see. You want the counterexample to be easy to verify, not exotic.

  • Need nonabelian? try \\(S_3\\) or \\(D_3\\).
  • Need abelian but not cyclic? try \\(\mathbb Z_2\times\mathbb Z_2\\).
  • Need non-normal subgroup? try \\(\langle(12)\rangle\le S_3\\).
  • Need equal-size but nonisomorphic groups? compare \\(\mathbb Z_4\\) and \\(\mathbb Z_2\times\mathbb Z_2\\).
  • Need relation failure? use a small finite set or a simple inequality relation on integers.

A good counterexample is minimal, explicit, and checked against the definition line by line. On homework and exams, the best answer is usually not the fanciest group; it is the smallest one that makes the false statement visibly fail.

Common Mistakes and Corrections

  • Mistake: forgetting to show an operation is well defined on equivalence classes. Correction: prove the result does not depend on the chosen representatives.
  • Mistake: treating closure alone as enough for a subgroup. Correction: use \(ab^{-1}\in H\), or the finite subgroup test when allowed.
  • Mistake: composing permutations left to right. Correction: apply the rightmost permutation first.
  • Mistake: assuming every divisor of \(|G|\) must occur as an element order or subgroup order. Correction: Lagrange gives necessary conditions, not always existence.
  • Mistake: thinking a homomorphism must be injective or must come from abelian groups. Correction: the only defining property is preserving the operation.
  • Mistake: writing quotient groups for arbitrary subgroups. Correction: only normal subgroups give quotient groups.
  • Mistake: confusing the kernel with the image. Correction: the kernel lives in the domain and maps to the identity; the image lives in the codomain and consists of values actually hit.
  • Mistake: claiming two groups are isomorphic because they have the same size. Correction: equal size alone is far from enough.

Counterexample habits that save time

  • Do not overbuild: if \\(S_3\\) works, do not reach for a much larger group.
  • Name the failed condition: say exactly whether closure, inverses, normality, well-definedness, or commutativity fails.
  • Use standard families: \\(\mathbb Z_n\\), direct products, \\(S_n\\), \\(A_n\\), and \\(D_n\\) should be your first tools.
  • Check the ambient operation: many bad counterexamples come from forgetting whether the group law is addition, multiplication, or composition.
